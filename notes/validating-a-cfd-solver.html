Validating a CFD solver against Ghia et al.

A solver that runs is not a solver that is correct. Here is what the lid-driven cavity benchmark actually checks, the bugs it caught in LG-FLOW, and why I turned it into a pass/fail gate instead of a figure I look at once.

Back to notes GitHub

The benchmark

The lid-driven cavity is the "hello world" of incompressible CFD validation: a square box of fluid with three fixed walls and a top lid that slides at constant velocity. It has no analytical solution, but Ghia, Ghia & Shin (1982) published high-resolution reference data for the steady velocity profiles along the cavity centerlines. Those tabulated profiles are the thing you compare against — at Re = 100 the flow is a single smooth vortex; at Re = 1000 secondary corner vortices appear and the centerline profiles sharpen, which is a much harsher test of the discretisation.

LG-FLOW is a 2D incompressible Navier–Stokes solver (finite volume, SIMPLE pressure–velocity coupling, staggered grid). "Validated" means its centerline u- and v-profiles match Ghia's reference to within fixed L2 and L∞ error thresholds at both Reynolds numbers.

What it caught

What the benchmark caught

The interesting part of validation is the failures. The benchmark earned its keep by catching the classic incompressible-solver bugs early — the kind that still produce a plausible-looking flow field:

  • Pressure–velocity decoupling. Get the grid arrangement wrong and you get a checkerboard pressure field that the velocity never feels — the picture looks fine, the centerline profile is quietly wrong. The staggered grid exists precisely to make this impossible.
  • Boundary-condition sign and indexing. A flipped lid direction or an off-by-one on the ghost cells barely changes the Re = 100 picture but pushes the Re = 1000 profiles out of tolerance — which is exactly why you validate at more than one Reynolds number.
  • Under-converged "steady" states. Stopping on iteration count instead of residual gives a field that looks converged and isn't. Tying the check to the L2 error against Ghia made that visible.

Make it a gate, not a figure

A benchmark you run by hand is a benchmark you stop running. So validation in LG-FLOW is an automated check: it computes the error metrics against the reference and returns a non-zero exit code if any metric falls outside tolerance — machine-readable, CI-friendly, no human in the loop.
